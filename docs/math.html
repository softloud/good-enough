2.3 Proof framework

To show this is true, we will need to:

  1. Define a structured intelligence system.
  2. Define the category ùí¢\mathcal G of structured intelligence governance.
  3. Define II as a digraph in terms of the category of structured intelligence governance.
  4. Define constraints on the morphisms of ùí¢\mathcal G in terms of II.
  5. Show SS is a discrete topological dynamical system, thus displays emergence.
  6. Show how II governs emergence in a structured intelligence system.

3 Structured intelligence systems

3.1 Definition: Structured intelligence system

A structured intelligence system SS is a directed graph S=(H‚à™A,M)S = (H \cup A, M) representing humans and automata interoperating toward some intention with objects and morphisms (unrigorously inspired by this paper I do not have access to (Badreddin and Jipp 2006)).

  • Nodes H‚à™AH \cup A are understood as collection of humans h‚ààHh \in H that interoperate with automata a‚ààAa \in A toward a human-defined intention.
  • Edge-relations MM representing the binary interoperations of the elements in the system:
    • hhhh: Humans talk, reflect, interact.
    • haha: Human instantiates heuristics on automata with intention.
    • aaaa: Automata stack without human intervention.
    • ahah: Automaton outputs results of heuristics applied to input.

3.3 Terms in SS

3.3.1 Definition: Humans

We invoke human in the conventional sense of the term and note that humans self-generate intention.

3.3.2 Definition: Intention

An intention requires human thought and is self-generating.

3.3.3 Definition: Automata

An automaton ar:I‚Ü¶Oa_r : I \mapsto O, with heuristics (rules written by humans) r‚ààRr \in R, transforms human intention i‚ààIi \in I and to some output o‚ààOo \in O. An automata does not have intention innately, only rules. For the purposes of this framework, we will generally think of an automataton aa as an object.

4 Governance

So, we now have automata interoperating with people. Think of The Matrix (Wachowski and Wachowski 1999) as a metaphor for our systems of technology in which humans are overwhemed by emergences. And, as Dr Salvin Calvin rightly observed in Robot Dreams (Asimov 1950), any robot in violation of the three laws must be immediately terminated.

4.1 Definition: The Three Laws of Robotics

As defined by Asimov in I, Robot (Asimov 1950).

  1. A robot may not injure a human being or, through inaction, allow a human being to come to harm.
  2. A robot must obey orders given it by human beings except where such orders would conflict with the First Law.
  3. A robot must protect its own existence as long as such protection does not conflict with the First or Second Law.

We must govern structured intelligence systems to ensure they do not harm humans. And we must learns from the metaphors, in particular the metonyms deconstructed in critical theory, to construct rigorous categorical frameworks that can be computationally and ethically applied to structural intelligence systems.

4.2 Definition: The three laws of structured intelligence governance

Adapted from the three laws.

  1. A structured intelligence system may not injure a human being or, through inaction, allow a human being to come to harm.
  2. A structured intelligence system must obey orders given it by human beings except where such orders would conflict with the First Law.
  3. A structured intelligence system must protect its own existence as long as such protection does not conflict with the First or Second Law.

This leads us to the question, how might we detect if a system is in violation of the laws?

5 Category of structured intelligence governance

The category (‚ÄúCategory Theory‚Äù 2025) ùí¢\mathcal G of structured intelligence governance is isomorphic to K2K_2 cycle with loops comprising:

7 Singularities

7.1 Definition: Technological singularity

A technololgical singularity is when the cycle of intelligence governance displays emergence beyond a constraint threshold for human governance. A singularity may be above a virtuous threshold, miscreant threshold, or a mix of both.

7.2 Definition: Singularity threshold

We will think emergence in terms of:

  1. time constraints;
  2. virtuous emergence;
  3. miscreant emergence.

7.3 Definition: Unbounded singularity

An unbounded singularity has no measure by which to check the laws; an unbounded singularity is in violation of the laws as we cannot confirm the system is not in violation.

A singularity in which virtuous E ‚â´\gg miscreant E, i.e., Œµv‚â´Œµm\varepsilon_v \gg \varepsilon_m, where burndown rate (measure of steps completed to estimated time) is above a certain Œ∏\theta threshold.

7.6 Definition: Singularity violation

A singularity in which there exists a violation of the laws, i.e., E=‚àûE = \infty.

7.7 Definition: Violation of the laws

  1. We say a singularity has violated Law 1. of the laws fo structured intelligence governance when there exist a non-empty subset of HH with emergence severity violation.

  2. We say a singularity has violated Law 2. when II contains elements of AA.

  3. We say a singularity has violated Law 3. when there are aaaa morphisms with unexpected output.
